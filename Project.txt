Introduction & Problem Statement
The Problem
Digital literacy remains a significant barrier for vulnerable populations, particularly the elderly, who are frequently targeted by phishing and SMS scams. Modern spam filters often operate as "black boxes"—they block messages without explaining why. This project aims to bridge that gap by providing a transparent Risk Score and identifying red flags in suspicious messages.

Why it Matters
Financial loss due to social engineering is a growing global issue. By creating a tool that assigns a probability-based score, we empower users to make informed decisions rather than relying solely on automated blocks.


2. Methodology & Approach
I followed the standard Machine Learning pipeline to build this solution:

A. Data CollectionI utilized a labeled dataset of SMS and Email messages (Spam vs. Ham).
    Source: [e.g., UCI Machine Learning Repository / Kaggle SMS Spam Collection].
    Size: [e.g., 5,574 messages].

B. Feature Engineering ($TF-IDF$)Raw text cannot be processed by machine learning models directly. I implemented Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) vectorization.
    Logic: This mathematical approach weighs words that are rare but significant (like "Urgent," "Account," "Suspended") more heavily than common stop-words (like "the," "is," "at").
    
C. Model Selection
I chose the Multinomial Naive Bayes (MNB) algorithm.

    Decision: MNB is highly effective for discrete features (like word counts) and is computationally efficient for text classification, making it ideal for a lightweight, real-time application.
    
    
3. Key Engineering Decisions

Stop-Word Removal: I filtered out common English words to reduce noise in the data, allowing the model to focus on intent-heavy keywords.

Probability vs. Binary Output: Instead of a simple "Safe/Unsafe" label, I extracted the underlying probability from the model to generate a 0-100% Risk Score. This provides more nuance for "borderline" messages.

Risk Thresholds: I set specific thresholds based on testing:
    < 40%: Low Risk
    40% - 70%: Caution (Requires human verification)
    > 70%: High Risk (Likely Phishing)
    
    
4. Challenges Faced

Data Imbalance: Most real-world datasets have far more "Safe" messages than "Phishing" ones. I had to ensure the model was sensitive enough to detect the "Phishing" minority without creating too many false alarms.

Short Texts: SMS messages provide very little context. To solve this, I ensured the $TF-IDF$ vectorizer considered "n-grams" (phrases) rather than just single words.
